Teach yourself enough to be a web developer in a year or less

What you’ll need
  • Purpose / Motivation
  • Why are you doing this?
  • Needs to be a driving force, one that will keep you going.
  • Have a clear goal
  • This could be as simple as “I want to make an app to keep track of my bills”.
  • It must be very specific.
  • Time
  • This one is tough especially if you have other obligations.
  • Make the time. Stay up an hour later to code if you have to.
  • I have a 22 mo old & work 40-60 hrs a week. To make time for this I literally had to make this my only  past time.
  • Choose 1 technology/language
  • It should align with your goal (for example: pick a mobile language/tech-stack such as Swift, Kotlin, or Flutter if your goal is a mobile app)
  • It's easy to get stuck trying to decide which language to start with.
  • It really doesn't matter as long as it is current.
  • Just pick one . More importantly - stick with it!
  • Ignore flavor of the week articles, pick established (boring - aka reliable) ones.

This is a deep and very dark rabbit hole. You will likely end up somewhere completely different than you could have ever imagined. That being said: proceed with clear intent  so as to not get lost in the labyrinth.

Take advantage of free knowledge before spending money on a course, book or system. You may even find that after you know more, you decide to go a completely different direction. I was certain that Ruby on Rails was for me, later I realized that C syntax languages made more sense to my brain.

Since this is about web development, you will want a good handle on HTML and CSS. A little JavaScript never hurt, but I would not invest too much time unless you plan on using JS Frameworks for your goal. You don't need to be an expert at HTML and CSS, but you need to understand the basics because you will always be using them one way or another. Luckily, there are many professional grade resources to start learning or to brush up your knowledge if it's been a while.

YouTube is a fountain of knowledge

  1. Traversy Media - Brad Traversy  - This guy is awesome. He has a video on how to start just about any language or technology. If I don't understand something (like git, npm, bootstrap or php) I go here and he has a video explaining it. He is beginner focused and doesn't complicate the subject matter by using technical jargon. I have 4 of his courses on Udemy.
  2. Acedemind - Maximilian Schwarzmüller - Max is a wonderful instructor. He is much more technical and thorough than Brad. I have purchased many of his courses.
  3. The Net Ninja  - Loads of very descriptive step by step tutorial series. Newer to the scene but already has a good size subscription base due to his easy to follow tutorials.
  4. Coding garden with CJ  - A new comer and a new favorite of mine. He does live shows and codes his heart out for hours on end. You get to follow along with non-scripted projects from scratch. A true example of how the process can be, mistakes and all. He can be a little difficult to follow at times so make sure you have already been exposed to the topics.
  5. Wes Bos  - Highly respected by his peers. Very unique projects in his code along tutorials. The way he is revered in the community, made me assume his stuff was next-level, so I honestly don't know much. His channel has some very interesting topics so maybe I am ready to see what the hype is all about!

The five above don't even scratch the surface. Be sure to follow the channel suggestions and subscribe on your favorites to broaden your horizons. You can learn a ton on YouTube. The only bad thing about YouTube is that it only goes so far. People will give out a ludicrous amount of knowledge but not quite all of it. Many of these guys above have links to their courses that you can buy which will have complete projects and much more in-depth material.

Free courses, tutorials & docs

W3schools  - This is what I did. I just started at HTML then CSS. I followed each link on list down the left of this site. For me, I just needed to be caught up to speed since it had been a while. Simply knowing what is possible, I can go back anytime to look up how. This is a huge reference with examples for everything. I'm always coming back here to remember how to do stuff.

Free Code Camp  - If you did this whole thing you'd be done. People get jobs after completing this!. I believe it as the curriculum appears to be solid and time consuming.

Learn Enough to Be Dangerous  - This guy has an amazing outlook on life and is training everyone who will listen. His techniques are fresh and he also provides the books in their entirety free of charge, on his site.

DevDocs.io  - All the docs in one place! I have a chrome extension for this one.

Scrimba  - Interactive tutorials. You can pause and change the code!

Scotch.io  - New to me but I will be going back soon. Half of courses are free. More than generous.

Paid courses

Udemy  - This is pretty popular right now but I have heard some some controversy lately so I don't know how long it'll continue to be a decent place to get courses. My only advice is make sure you pay attention to the ratings also look at how much content you get for the price. I am not 💯 positive, but it sure seems like $14.99 is the highest they charge for a course (don’t let the crossed out price fool you) and the lowest is $9.99. Wait for the sales. They always come back. I have heard that this course by Colt Steele has everything you need if you are looking for a good one to start with. I have his course on SQL and he knows his stuff. Also I don’t know where else you can get 42+ hours of video instruction, the value is insane.
